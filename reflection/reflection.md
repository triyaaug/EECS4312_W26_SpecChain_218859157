# Application: [Head Space]

- Most important difference between pipelines: [The automated pipeline was fast and produced broad personas, but they were sometimes vague or misclassified. The manual pipeline gave more focused personas grounded in actual review groups, but it was slower and could be inconsistent across reviewers. The hybrid pipeline ended up being the best of both, it used automation for speed and traceability, then manual refinement fixed errors, clarified personas, and made sure everything actually matched the reviews.]

- Most useful pipeline: [The hybrid pipeline gave the most balanced outputs. It kept the traceability of automated results, made sure personas were tied to the right review groups, and produced requirements that were clear, actionable, and testable. Refining the automated results helped avoid vague descriptions and unsupported assumptions while still saving time.]

- Most surprising finding: [Some personas included assumptions not backed by the reviews. Positive experience reviews, for instance, were first labeled as “Content and Features,” which didn’t really reflect the user sentiment. Automated outputs also sometimes produced overlapping or redundant requirements, which manual review helped clean up.]

- Observed weakness in the automated pipeline: [Automation is great for speed and traceability, but it can miss nuance and context. Manual refinement is slower but makes outputs more accurate and clear. The hybrid approach struck a good balance, giving personas and requirements that were clear, traceable, and based on actual reviews, while still saving time compared to fully manual work. Starting with automation and doing targeted manual fixes feels like the best way to go.]
